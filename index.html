Get That Water!

Get That Water! is a critical design board game that shows the uneven power dynamic between corporations, and average person trying to seek water. In the game, one player is a Corporation and the other is a Water Seeker. Each turn, the player picks up an action card according to the role they are playing as. Action cards illustrate real life water-issue scenarios like corporations polluting water sources, corporations buying out water sources, and in turn people no longer being able to access water. Additionally, there are four water source spaces; tap water, natural spring water, lake water, and ocean water that players can gain control of. The Water Seeker is never able to win the game in order to demonstrate the real life power dynamic between corporations and average citizens.

Today I Am Going To

At the start of this project, most people had been forced to quarantine and self-isolate due to the COVID-19 outbreak. My HCI final project team took this as an opportunity to create a web app to keep people from being bored. This interface was designed to provide challenge and activity suggestions to keep people busy at home. These activities covered a wide range of categories, including exercise, create, build, write, draw, and garden. Each category contains a list of three activity suggestions. For example, the exercise grouping contains different workouts like yoga, (virtual) dance classes, and online programs. Users can access any of these lists from the home page. From their desired category list, the user can add specific activities to their own list. By doing this, the user can then keep track of what they plan to do or what they have already completed.


Project Management


For this group project, I acted as the Project Manager and decided to impliment a Scrum system using Trello. Throughout the work process, we implimented four one week long design sprints: Preliminart Research & Planning, Design, Implimentation, and Polish. In addition, I had the team utilize the Github powerup in order to manage our code review.


Formative Evaluation


In order to provide the best and most useful experience possible, we designed a formative evaluation that we completed through the method of designing and sending out a survey. The questions in this evaluation are designed to not only see how much our audience's screen time has increased during quarentine but to also see if they are completing methods to reduce that.


Persona


In order to best guide our design for this application, we developed a persona who is young adult who just lost his job and loves to exercise. Through developing this persona, we hoped to cater this application to someone like Allen who needs a stress reliever after searching for new work.


Mood Board


In this moodboard, we wanted to gather potential color palettes and stylistic choices to our application. Since being in quarantine is a very difficult situation for so many, we wanted to encapsulate a softer, less saturated color style. To do this, we took inspiration from the soft green tones in nature, as well as the pastel colors used in flat design illustratrations. From the nature and flat color illustrations about productivity, we ended up settling on these five different colors to shape our color palette. Another part of the moodboard that we implemented was the minimalist design of the icons, clock, smiley face, and illustrations. At the moment, life is cluttered with constant emotions of being overwhelmed with the current situation we are facing as a society. Through utilizing a minimal design with soft colors, we hope to promote a calm yet productive feeling towards our users.
